Before I continue, I want to honor Ian Stirling, who turned out to be an epoch in polar bear research and left a legacy of scientific publications and books, even in Russia.

Let’s start from map with north pole sighting from central Russia

Russian territories and adjusted aquatories cross with boundaries of 4 PB subopulation: BS, KS, LV, CS. Western, BS, is shared with Norway. Eastern, CS, is shared with United States. Bilateral studies of these outer populations are possible both in collaboration and independently. It is the responsibility of Russia to study the internal subpopulations.

All Russian sipop belong to “Divergent ice” ecoregion.

According to the Status Table as revised on October 2023, population size is unknown of Kara and Laptes seas. For BS population size is about 2600 with reference to 2004. For CS population size is about 3000 with reference to 2016.

Distance sampling was used to estimate population size in the Barents sea, and capture-recapture with density extrapolation method was used for population size estimation in the Chukchi Sea. Hope, in Friday Peter Boveng will provide independent assessment after ChESS project.

It is considered, that long-term change in subpopulation size is positive for BS. Statements for other ones are impossible due to data defitient.

For short-term period during one generation length both outer subpopulations are likely stable. No enough data for statements about inner subpopulations.

Sea ice metrics such as trends in date of the spring advance and in date of the fall advance as well as changing of the summer sea ice area are great for all subpopulation. Phenological chages are more crusial for BS. Summer sea ice retreat is more significant for CS.

New regulations

Under the Red Data Book (Red book, 2001), polar bears in Russia are segregated into three different populations: Kara-Barents Sea population, Laptev Sea population, Alaska-Chukotka population.

Presently Ministry in its statements and official documents uses term “subpopulation” “population” for consistence with IUCN definitions.

ArcNet

ArcNet is a network design framework to help achieve ocean-scale, area-based marine conservation in the Arctic. The framework is centred around a network of 83 priority areas for conservation (PACs) designed through expert-driven systematic conservation planning using conservation targets for over 800 conservation features (CFs) that represent Arctic biodiversity. These CFs, their targets, and PACs were identified systematically based on their biological and ecological significance; therefore, implementing the ArcNet PACs as a network of protected and conserved areas will strengthen the Arctic Ocean’s biodiversity and help nations deliver on their UN-level commitments to protect 30% of oceans by 2030 (Convention on Biological Diversity, 2022).

James et al. (2024)

Regarding to polar bear conservation the ArcNet project should be mentioned. There were many data contributors, especially from Russia, who provied spatial data of distributions, feeding and breeding area and many other parameters for pinnipeds, cetacens, benthos, fihhes, birds, polar bear. Parameters of sea ice and coastal zones are also included. Based on weights and targets the network of 83 priority areas for conservation was defined. Results are published in the early 2024.

Authors applied ecosystem approach to monitoring marine mammals the Laptev Sea and gave some conclusion and recommendations for the Russian Arctic.

Laptev Sea

  • Habitat changes has a negative impact to marine mammals and polar bear.

  • In the case of reducing of prey availability the walk activity is increased, and this leads to raising of energy lost, and energy balance cannot be replenished by prey lack

  • Warming favours the destruction of maternal dens earlier than normal emergence from dens.

  • And lastly, increasing cases of conflicts between polar bears and human are due to insufficiently prey resources.
